# Product

## Register

product

## Users

UHC serves students and staff patients, doctors, admins, and super admins inside a university health center. Patients use it to book appointments, manage health-center visits, view documents, receive reminders, and update profile or notification settings. Doctors use it during clinical workflows to review schedules, manage appointments, confirm attendance with QR check-in, update appointment status, and see patient context. Admins use it for operational management: departments, doctors, users, analytics, reports, and permission-aware workflows. Super admins use it for governance: admin role control, slot enforcement, permission assignment, and audit review.

## Product Purpose

UHC exists to make university healthcare easier to access, manage, and govern from one multilingual Flutter application. Success means students and staff can book care without friction, doctors can act on the right appointment information at the right time, admins can run the health center with confidence, and super admins can maintain a clear trust boundary with auditability. The product should feel dependable in everyday patient use and precise during high-responsibility administrative work.

## Brand Personality

Calm, trustworthy, modern, and operationally clear. The interface should feel clinical enough for healthcare, warm enough for university users, and disciplined enough for admin governance. Its voice should be direct, helpful, and reassuring, especially around errors, permissions, appointment states, and destructive actions.

## Anti-references

Do not make UHC feel like a generic hospital template with only white, teal, and stock medical icons everywhere. Do not make it feel playful in a way that makes healthcare workflows unserious. Do not make it feel like a heavy corporate admin system with dense gray tables and no warmth. Do not use decorative glass, gradients, or effects when they reduce scan speed, readability, or confidence.

## Design Principles

1. Make trust visible: role boundaries, appointment states, errors, and admin actions should be obvious before the user commits.
2. Serve the workflow first: patient booking, doctor scheduling, admin management, and super-admin governance should be fast to scan and hard to misread.
3. Keep both themes first-class: light mode and dark mode are both required product experiences and must be supported through shared tokens.
4. Use warmth with restraint: the app can feel modern and polished, but decoration must never compete with medical or governance decisions.
5. Respect multilingual use: English, Arabic, and Kurdish interfaces need layouts, copy, and spacing that survive RTL and longer translated strings.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Design and implementation should target accessible healthcare workflows for mobile, web, and desktop users. Maintain strong contrast in both light and dark themes, avoid color-only status communication, support readable text scaling, preserve touch targets appropriate for mobile use, and make critical states clear for users who may be stressed, tired, or working quickly. Motion should remain purposeful and non-blocking, with reduced-motion-friendly alternatives where animation is used.
